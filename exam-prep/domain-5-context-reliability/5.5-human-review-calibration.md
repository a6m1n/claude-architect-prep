# 5.5 — Design human review workflows and confidence calibration

> **Domain 5: Context Management & Reliability** (15%) · **Task Statement 5.5** | **Scenarios:** Structured Data Extraction | **What it tests:** whether you can avoid trusting aggregate accuracy, stratify the metric by segment, calibrate confidence on a labeled set, and route review before you automate.

## ⚡ TL;DR

- **The aggregate lies.** "97% overall accuracy" can hide a segment (a document type or a specific field) where the model fails. Before you automate anything — **break the metric down by document type and by field**.
- **Calibrate confidence, don't trust the raw number.** `field-level confidence` only means something after calibration on a **labeled validation set**, so that "high confidence" maps to a known error rate.
- **Stratified sampling, not uniform.** For ongoing error measurement, take a **stratified random sampling** of high-confidence extractions — otherwise a rare risky segment won't appear in the sample.
- **Validate first, then automate.** Only reduce review on the segments that provably hold their accuracy. Low-confidence and contradictory/ambiguous documents — always to a human.
- **Principle under the hood:** root-cause-over-symptom (the aggregate is a symptom, the segment error is the cause) + matching workload to mechanism (point the limited reviewer capacity at where the risk is).

## 🧠 In plain words

> **Analogy:** 🌡️ the average temperature across a hospital. "36.6 overall" hides the ward where everyone has a fever — measure by ward, not with one shared thermometer.
>
> A single number, "97% accuracy", is an average over all documents. It can hide a document type or a field where the model fails (for example receipts — 99%, contracts — 70%, yet the average still looks nice). So BEFORE you automate, break the accuracy down **by document type and by field**, and first calibrate the raw `confidence` on a labeled set (`labeled validation set` — where you know the correct answers). Low-confidence and contradictory documents — always to a human.
>
> 🚩 **Detector:** if the answer is "overall accuracy is high, automate everything" — that's **true-but-irrelevant** (true, but not about the weak segment); the right move is to stratify the metric by segment and reduce review only where accuracy provably holds.
>
> 🧷 **Reflex:** don't trust the average — measure by ward (type × field), and send anything contested or uncertain → to a human.

## 📘 Theory

### Why aggregate accuracy is dangerous
A single overall figure (`97% overall`) is an **average over a heterogeneous population**. If 95% of the flow is simple receipts at 99% accuracy and 5% is complex contracts at 70% accuracy, the average still looks great, and the contract failure "drowns out". Automating based on an undecoded average means quietly shipping to production exactly the segment that breaks. That's why the official task requires you to **validate accuracy by document type and by field segment** before any automation. This is a direct example of the *root-cause-over-symptom* principle: the aggregate is a symptom of pipeline health, while the segment breakdown reveals the cause.

### Field-level confidence must be calibrated
The model can output a `confidence` for each field, but the **raw** number has no fixed relationship to the real error frequency: "0.9" on one field can mean 2% errors, on another — 25%. Calibration means: you take a **labeled validation set** (where you know the correct answers), match the predicted confidence against the actual accuracy, and set thresholds so that "high confidence" guarantees an acceptable error rate. The official docs on building evaluations explicitly insist on a **held-out / labeled test set** and measurable criteria (F1, accuracy) — this is exactly the foundation of calibration.

### Stratified sampling for ongoing monitoring
After automation you can't "forget" about the segments. **Stratified random sampling** splits the flow into strata (document type × field) and samples from each one, guaranteeing that even a rare segment is observed. You need this so as to (a) measure the error rate in the high-confidence extractions specifically and (b) catch **novel error patterns** (drift, new formats). Uniform sampling doesn't give you this: it is proportional to volume, so a rare high-risk segment almost never appears in the sample.

### Routing the limited reviewer capacity
Reviewer capacity is a scarce resource (*matching workload to mechanism*). The rule: send to a human the cases with **low model confidence** and **ambiguous / contradictory source documents**, prioritizing exactly those; automate the high-confidence, provably accurate segments, leaving a stratified sample on top for audit. Human review remains critical as a guardrail — we don't remove it entirely, we aim it at the risk.

## ⚠️ Traps and distractors

| Trap archetype | Why it's wrong |
| --- | --- |
| **Trusting the aggregate** ("97% is great, automate everything") | The average hides the worst segment. The failing field will go to production unnoticed. The metric must be decomposed `by document type` and `by field` before automation. |
| **Raw (uncalibrated) confidence** | Without calibration on a labeled set the threshold isn't tied to real accuracy: "high confidence" can still be frequently wrong. You need a **labeled validation set**. |
| **Uniform random sampling** | Sampling is right as an idea, but a uniform sample is proportional to volume and blind to rare risky segments. You need a **stratified** sample by segment. |
| **Automating before per-segment validation** | Reducing review is only justified on segments that provably hold their accuracy. Without per-segment validation it's a blind bet. |
| **"Let's remove review entirely, keep only flags"** | Removing human-in-the-loop completely breaks the guardrail; contradictory/ambiguous documents must also go to a human, even if the model is "confident". |

## 🔑 Remember

- Decompose accuracy **by document type AND by field segment** — never trust a single `overall` number.
- Calibrate `field-level confidence` on a **labeled validation set** (held-out test set); the threshold must map to a known error rate.
- Ongoing monitoring = **stratified random sampling** of high-confidence extractions → measures errors + catches novel patterns.
- To a human: **low confidence** OR **ambiguous/contradictory** documents; prioritize the limited reviewer capacity.
- Automate a segment **only after** proven per-segment accuracy.

## 📝 Practice

*Answer on your own first, then expand the "Answer".*

**Q1 · easy.** An extraction pipeline for invoices, contracts, and receipts reports 97% overall field accuracy. The team wants to reduce human review. What should they do FIRST?

A) Trust the 97% figure and remove human review across all document types and fields.
B) Break the accuracy down by document type and by field, and only reduce review on segments that prove consistently accurate.
C) Switch to a larger model so the aggregate number rises above 99%.
D) Keep reviewing 100% of all extractions indefinitely, since automation is too risky.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: B.** The aggregate can hide a failing segment, so first we decompose the metric `by document type` and `by field` and reduce review only where accuracy provably holds.
- A — the "trust the aggregate" trap: quietly automates the worst segment.
- C — swaps the root cause for a symptom: a higher average doesn't guarantee the weak segment is fixed.
- D — ignores the task (optimize reviewer capacity) and doesn't use per-segment validation.
</details>

**Q2 · medium.** The team wants to route extractions to humans automatically. They plan to send everything where the model's raw confidence score is below 0.8. What is the main weakness of this approach?

A) Confidence scores cannot be produced at the field level, only per document.
B) Raw confidence is uncalibrated, so a fixed 0.8 cutoff has no known relationship to the real error rate; thresholds must be calibrated on a labeled validation set.
C) Routing by confidence is inherently wrong; review should be uniform-random instead.
D) Below 0.8 is too low; any threshold under 0.95 is unsafe for every field.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: B.** Raw confidence isn't calibrated: 0.8 on different fields corresponds to different actual accuracy. The threshold needs to be calibrated on a **labeled validation set** so it maps to a known error rate.
- A — factually wrong: confidence can be produced at the field level (`field-level`).
- C — throws away a working mechanism; uniform sampling solves a different problem (audit), not routing.
- D — an invented universal "0.95": the right threshold depends on the field and is determined by calibration, not by a magic number.
</details>

**Q3 · hard.** High-confidence extractions are now automated. To keep monitoring quality over time, the team will audit a sample of these automated extractions. Most volume is simple receipts; contracts are rare but high-stakes. Which sampling strategy best detects emerging errors?

A) Uniform random sampling across all automated extractions at a fixed rate.
B) Sample only the receipts, since they make up most of the volume.
C) Stratified random sampling across document-type/field segments, so rare segments like contracts are observed and novel error patterns surface.
D) Stop sampling automated extractions; they are high-confidence and were already validated once.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: C.** A stratified sample guarantees that every segment (including the rare contracts) is observed, measures the error rate in the high-confidence flow, and catches novel patterns/drift.
- A — uniform is proportional to volume: the rare high-risk contracts will almost never appear in the sample.
- B — concentrates on the simplest segment, ignoring exactly the one that carries the risk.
- D — a one-time validation doesn't protect against drift and new formats; monitoring must be ongoing.
</details>

### Tricky question **

**Q★★ (tricky):** Per-field, calibrated confidence is in place. For the `total_amount` field on contracts, calibration on a labeled validation set shows that confidence ≥ 0.95 corresponds to 99.5% accuracy — comfortably above the automation bar. A reviewer notices that several contracts being auto-approved at confidence 0.97 contain **internally contradictory totals** (the summary page and the line-item page disagree). What is the BEST response?

A) Trust the calibration: 0.97 ≥ 0.95 maps to a 99.5% accuracy band, so these are within the expected error budget — no action needed.
B) Lower the automation threshold for `total_amount` on all contracts from 0.95 to 0.99 to be safe.
C) Add a deterministic routing rule that sends contracts with contradictory/ambiguous source content to human review regardless of confidence, and treat the missed cases as a signal to re-examine the validation set's coverage.
D) Discard field-level confidence entirely and go back to reviewing 100% of contracts.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: C.** Two principles of the task converge here. The calibration is correct *on average*, but the task separately requires routing **ambiguous / contradictory source documents** to a human — this is an independent trigger, **not** replaceable by high confidence. A contradictory document (summary ≠ line-items) is about the quality of the *source*, not about the model's confidence; high confidence on a contradictory input is exactly the dangerous case. The right solution is a deterministic routing rule on contradiction/ambiguity independent of confidence, plus a signal that the validation set apparently didn't cover such contracts well enough (otherwise calibration would have "seen" them).

Why the others are traps:
- A — "trust the calibration/aggregate" in a new wrapper: 99.5% is a band on a labeled set that clearly under-represented contradictory contracts; averaged accuracy doesn't justify skipping an obvious contradictory signal.
- B — treats the symptom with a threshold: nudging 0.95→0.99 doesn't solve the contradictory *input* problem (the model is confident even at 0.97), but it does hit reviewer capacity on the correct contracts.
- D — overcorrection: throws away working calibration and breaks the prioritization of the limited reviewer capacity; you need a targeted rule, not a return to 100% manual review.
</details>

# 5.2 — Design effective escalation and ambiguity resolution patterns

> **Domain 5: Context Management & Reliability** (15%) · **Task Statement 5.2** | **Scenarios:** Customer Support Resolution Agent | **What it tests:** when an agent must hand control to a human (escalation) and how to resolve ambiguity (several matches → ask for an identifier), without replacing the real triggers with weak proxies.

## ⚡ TL;DR

- Escalate on **three real triggers**: (1) the customer clearly asks for a human, (2) a gap or exception in the policy (policy gap), (3) the agent cannot make meaningful progress. The agent handles everything else on its own.
- If the customer **clearly demands a human** — escalate **immediately**, without an upfront "investigation."
- **Sentiment** (tone, frustration) and **self-reported confidence** (`confidence 1-10`) are **bad proxies** for case difficulty; do not tie escalation to them.
- **Policy gap ≠ hard case.** The agent handles a hard but policy-covered case on its own; escalation is needed where the rule is **silent or contradictory** (example: a competitor price-match when the policy only covers your own site).
- **Several customer matches** → ask for an extra identifier (`user_id`, email, order #); **do not heuristically pick** one of the candidates.

## 📘 Theory

### The three legitimate escalation triggers

Task 5.2 names exactly three situations in which the agent must call `escalate_to_human`:

1. **Explicit human request.** The customer directly asks for an operator/manager. This is about customer autonomy, not task difficulty.
2. **Policy exception / policy gap.** The agent has all the facts, but the governing rule is missing, silent, or contradictory. The agent has **no right to invent a policy** that the organization never defined.
3. **No meaningful progress possible.** The agent has exhausted the available tools/data and cannot safely move toward a resolution.

The principle behind this is **least privilege / authority boundary**: an autonomous agent acts only within the authority it has been given. When a decision requires *interpreting or extending* a rule that does not exist, the agent has no authority — that is the zone for human judgment. In *Building effective agents*, Anthropic directly recommends building in points to "pause for human feedback at checkpoints or when encountering blockers."

### Escalate immediately vs. resolve on your own

- **The customer clearly demands a human → escalate right away.** Do not first run `get_customer`/`lookup_order` "to figure things out." An upfront investigation ignores the customer's clearly stated wish and wastes time.
- **A simple case within the agent's abilities → resolve it yourself.** Acknowledge the customer's frustration, but escalate only if the customer **repeats** the demand. Escalating "just in case" because the conversation is awkward is *emotional avoidance*, not an operational necessity.

### Why sentiment and confidence are bad proxies

This is the key trap of this domain. The real difficulty of a case **does not correlate** with either the customer's tone or the model's self-assessment:

- **Sentiment**: a hard policy-exception case can come from a completely calm customer, while a routine replacement from a photo can come from a furious one. Tying to tone misroutes in both directions.
- **Self-reported confidence**: the model is poorly calibrated and easily "fools" itself — an agent that confidently misreads a policy exception will report high confidence on that very case.

The root cause of inverted escalation ("easy ones go out, hard ones stay in") is an **unclear decision boundary**. The correct first intervention is the most **proportionate** one: add explicit escalation criteria + few-shot examples to the system prompt (root-cause-over-symptom), rather than bolting on a classifier or a confidence threshold.

### Ambiguity: several matches → ask for an identifier

If a customer search returned several records, the agent should **ask for a clarifying identifier** rather than picking a candidate by heuristic (for example, "I'll take the most recent one"). This is part of context engineering and tool design: *Writing effective tools for agents* advises naming parameters unambiguously (`user_id`, not `user`) and returning **actionable errors** that tell the agent which input is needed. A heuristic pick = a silent error that leads to actions on someone else's account.

## ⚠️ Traps and distractors

| Trap archetype | Why it's wrong |
| --- | --- |
| **Escalating on negative sentiment** | Tone is a weak proxy for difficulty; a calm customer may carry a policy exception, while an angry one may have a trivial replacement. Misrouting in both directions. |
| **Escalating on low self-reported confidence** | The model is poorly calibrated; confidently-wrong cases get a high self-score. A `confidence < N` threshold catches the wrong cases. |
| **Escalating "because the case is hard"** | Difficulty ≠ policy gap. If the policy covers the case, the agent must carry out the procedure itself, even if it is multi-step. We escalate when the rule is *missing/silent*, not when it is laborious. |
| **Heuristically picking one of several matches** | Silently acts on what may be someone else's account. The right move is to ask for an extra identifier. |
| **"Investigating" first even though the customer clearly demands a human** | Ignores the customer's clear wish; immediate escalation is mandatory. Investigation is appropriate only when the customer did NOT demand a human. |
| **Escalating to avoid an awkward but factually clear conversation** | *Emotional avoidance*: when the facts and a procedure exist (e.g. tracking shows delivery), the agent should act, not hide behind a human. |

## 🔑 Remember

- Real escalation triggers: **explicit human request · policy gap/exception · no meaningful progress**.
- An explicit demand for a human → **escalate immediately**, without an upfront investigation.
- **Sentiment** and **self-reported confidence score** are NOT triggers (poorly calibrated).
- **Policy gap ≠ complexity.** A hard but covered case → we resolve it ourselves.
- Several matches → **ask for an identifier** (`user_id`/email/order #), do not guess.
- The first fix for inverted escalation: **explicit criteria + few-shot in the system prompt**, not a classifier and not a confidence threshold.

## 📝 Practice

*Answer first yourself, then expand "Answer".*

**Q1 · easy.** A customer messages your support agent: "Stop talking to me, I want a real person right now." Tooling for `get_customer` and `lookup_order` is available. What should the agent do?

A) Run `get_customer` and `lookup_order` first to gather context, then decide whether escalation is warranted.
B) Immediately call `escalate_to_human` and hand off to a person.
C) Ask the customer to describe the problem so the agent can try to resolve it autonomously.
D) Run a sentiment check; escalate only if frustration exceeds the threshold.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: B.** The customer **explicitly demanded a human** — this is a legitimate trigger, so we escalate **immediately**. A is the "investigate first" trap: gathering data upfront ignores the customer's clear wish. C tries to resolve it on its own despite a direct demand. D is the sentiment-proxy trap: escalation here is determined by the explicit request, not by a tone threshold.
</details>

**Q2 · medium.** Searching the CRM for the caller returns three customer records with the same name but different account IDs. What is the most appropriate next step?

A) Pick the record with the most recent activity, since that is most likely the active customer.
B) Escalate to a human because multiple matches indicate a complex case.
C) Ask the customer for an additional identifier (e.g., email or order number) to disambiguate the match.
D) Proceed with all three accounts in parallel and let the customer correct any mistake.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: C.** Several matches → **ask for a clarifying identifier**; this is resolving ambiguity, not a reason to act blindly. A is a heuristic pick of a candidate (you might act on someone else's account). B is a false "by complexity" escalation: several matches are not a policy gap, and the agent is perfectly able to ask for an identifier itself. D acts on other people's accounts; "the customer will correct it" shifts the risk onto the user.
</details>

**Q3 · hard.** A customer asks for a price match against a competitor's lower price. Your policy explicitly allows price adjustments for drops on your **own** site within 14 days but says nothing about competitor pricing. The agent has already retrieved all order and customer data. What is the most appropriate action?

A) Apply the price match, since the 14-day adjustment policy is the closest applicable rule.
B) Decline the request outright, because the policy does not list competitor pricing.
C) Escalate to a human for policy interpretation, since the policy is silent on competitor price matching.
D) Escalate because the customer is likely frustrated about the price difference.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: C.** This is a genuine **policy gap**: the rule is silent about competitors, and the agent has no right to invent a policy — human interpretation/extension of the rule is needed. A is fabricating policy: the "closest rule" cannot be applied, it is about your own site. B is also a self-made decision (a refusal) on a nonexistent rule; in a gap the agent does not decide either way, it escalates. D is the sentiment trap: the basis for escalation is the gap in the policy, not the assumed frustration.
</details>

### Tricky question **

**Q★★ (tricky):** A customer claims they never received their order. Tracking shows it was delivered and signed for at their address three days ago. Separately, your refund policy is fully specified for confirmed non-delivery but does not address disputed-delivery cases like this one. The customer is calm and has NOT asked for a human. What should the agent do?

A) Escalate to a human, because the policy is silent on disputed-delivery cases — this is a genuine policy gap.
B) Share the tracking facts (delivered, signed for, date) with the customer per standard procedure, and proceed; escalate only if it then becomes a true exception or the customer requests a person.
C) Issue the refund, applying the closest matching rule (the confirmed-non-delivery refund policy).
D) Run sentiment analysis; since the customer is calm, close the ticket as resolved.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: B.** Here two principles collide, and that is the catch. It is tempting to pick A, because "the policy is silent on disputed-delivery" formally sounds like the policy gap from Q3. But before declaring a gap, the agent has **concrete facts and a standard procedure**: show the customer the tracking data (delivered, signed for, date). This is not "investigating despite a demand for a human" (the customer did not demand one) and not emotional avoidance — on the contrary, escalating to avoid an awkward but factually backed conversation is itself avoidance. We escalate only if, after presenting the facts, the case really does turn into an exception.

Why the others are traps: **A** — premature escalation: the gap trigger applies when the agent has no facts/procedure, but here it does. **C** — fabrication: applying the "closest rule" (confirmed non-delivery) to a disputed delivery is not allowed; that is inventing policy. **D** — the sentiment trap inside out: the customer being calm does not mean the issue is resolved; tone determines neither escalation nor closing the ticket.
</details>

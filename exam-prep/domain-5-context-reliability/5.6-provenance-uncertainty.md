# 5.6 — Preserve information provenance and handle uncertainty in multi-source synthesis

> **Domain 5: Context Management & Reliability** (15%) · **Task Statement 5.6** | **Scenarios:** Multi-Agent Research System | **What it tests:** the ability to keep the "claim → source" link through every compression step, mark conflicts with attribution, carry dates through, and render different content types differently.

## ⚡ TL;DR

- **Provenance dies in summarization.** When a subagent compresses findings, it silently loses the "claim → source" link. The architecture must *force* subagents to emit **structured claim-source mappings** (source URL, document name, relevant excerpt), and downstream agents must keep and merge them without breaking the link.
- **Conflict ≠ choice.** If two credible sources give different numbers, do not pick one, do not average, do not drop one. **Annotate the conflict with attribution** and leave the decision to the coordinator (it distinguishes well-established vs contested).
- **Dates are mandatory.** Carry publication/collection dates into the structured output: otherwise a time difference ($4.2B in 2023 vs $5.1B in 2024) reads as a contradiction.
- **Render content types in their own way:** financial data → tables, news → prose, technical findings → structured lists. Do not flatten everything into one format.
- The top-level principle: **least lossy synthesis** — every compression step must maximize recall (keep everything important), and only then surgically remove what is redundant.

## 🧠 In plain words

> **Analogy:** 📰 a journalist puts a source next to EVERY number; when two sources disagree, they cite both with a reference instead of silently picking a favorite.
>
> When a subagent compresses findings into smooth text, every fact silently loses its "where did this come from" (the claim-source mapping). Force it to return the structure `{claim, source_url, document_name, excerpt, date}`, and have the next agent keep it instead of retelling it. Two reliable sources gave different numbers — that is no reason to pick one: present both with attribution and **dates** (without a date, "$4.2B in 2023 vs $5.1B in 2024" reads as a lie, when it is just different points in time). The coordinator decides.
>
> 🚩 **Detector:** if an answer suggests "average them / take the newer one / take the larger one / remove the citations for cleanliness" — that is **symptom-not-root-cause** (or fabricating a number); the right move is to annotate both values with source and date, and hand reconciliation to the coordinator.
>
> 🧷 **Reflex:** a source next to every number · a date is mandatory · on conflict, annotate both, do not pick · each type in its own format.

## 📘 Theory

### Why provenance is lost (root cause)

A multi-agent research system is an orchestrator-workers pattern: the lead agent hands out subtasks to subagents, they search in parallel, and then the lead **compresses** their findings into a single report. The point of failure is exactly this compression step. According to the official context-engineering guide, *"overly aggressive compaction can result in the loss of subtle but critical context"*. Applied to research: during summarization the first thing to fall away is the **claim-source mapping** — the text of the claim stays, but the "where did this come from" disappears. The report looks clean but becomes unverifiable.

The fix is not "write a better prompt about citations" but to change the **contract between agents**: a subagent must return the structure `{claim, source_url, document_name, excerpt, date}`, and downstream agents must **keep and merge** it rather than retell it. In Anthropic's real system this responsibility was moved into a separate `CitationAgent` that checks that every claim is actually tied to a source.

### Conflicting statistics: annotate, do not decide

When two **credible** sources give different values, the analysis stage has no authority to silently reconcile them. Its job is to **surface the conflict with attribution**. Reconciliation is a deliberate downstream decision for the coordinator/report, which can separate **well-established findings** from **contested findings**. This is an application of the principle of **least privilege to decisions**: whoever reads a document should not make the editorial decision of "which number is correct".

### Temporal data

Often a "contradiction" is just different points of measurement. That is why the publication/collection **date** must travel into the structured output. With both dated numbers, the coordinator can see: this is not a contradiction but a temporal trend. Without a date, it looks like two credible sources "lying about each other".

### Rendering by content type

The final report must not flatten everything into one format. Different types of information carry different structure: **financial data → tables**, **news → prose**, **technical findings → structured lists**. Converting everything into one format destroys the structure and part of the meaning (for example, a table rewritten as prose loses precision).

## ⚠️ Traps and distractors

| Trap archetype | Why it's wrong |
|---|---|
| **Drop citations during summarization** ("let's make the summary cleaner") | This is the main failure mode. A "clean" summary without a claim-source mapping = an unverifiable report. Provenance must survive *every* compression step. |
| **Pick the most recent value** (drop the older number) | Dates are for *interpreting* values, not for auto-deleting one. It throws away a credible data point and hides the conflict from the coordinator. |
| **Average the conflicting values** | Fabricates a number that *no source reported*. Creates false confidence and erases the methodology of both sources. |
| **Pick the higher / "not understate"** | Introduces bias and destroys provenance under the excuse of a "clean summary". A classic summarization loss of attribution. |
| **Omit dates** | A temporal difference reads as a contradiction; the report loses the ability to tell a trend from an error. |
| **Uniform format for everything** | Flattening tables/prose/lists into one format loses structure and precision; financial data suffers most. |
| **"Improve the subagent's prompt so it does not forget citations"** | Treats the symptom, not the contract. Reliability comes from a **structured output schema** + a downstream duty to keep it, not from a polite request in the prompt. |

## 🔑 Remember

- Subagent → **structured claim-source mapping**: `source_url`, `document_name`, `excerpt`, `date`. Downstream **preserve + merge**, do not retell.
- Conflict of credible sources → **annotate with attribution**, the **coordinator** does the reconciliation.
- The report separates **well-established** vs **contested** findings.
- **Dates are mandatory** in the structured output → temporal diff ≠ contradiction.
- Render by type: **financial → tables**, **news → prose**, **technical → structured lists**. No uniform format.
- Never **average** and never **silently drop** credible values.

## 📝 Practice

*Answer on your own first, then expand "Answer".*

**Q1 · easy**
A research subagent finishes analyzing 12 source documents and returns only a fluent paragraph summarizing the findings, with no URLs or excerpts. The synthesis agent then merges several such paragraphs into the final report. What is the root-cause architectural flaw?

A) The subagents should run sequentially instead of in parallel.
B) The subagent output contract drops claim-source mappings during summarization, making findings unverifiable downstream.
C) The synthesis agent uses too small a context window.
D) The coordinator should average the findings to reduce noise.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: B.** The root cause is the subagent's output contract: it compresses findings into prose and loses the "claim → source" link. Provenance must survive summarization; the fix is a structured claim-source mapping (`source_url`, `document_name`, `excerpt`).
A — parallelism is irrelevant here (a distracting process factor). C — context size is not the cause of the lost attribution (wrong symptom). D — *average* fabricates a number and is itself a trap archetype.
</details>

**Q2 · medium**
Two credible market reports disagree: Report A says the segment is $4.2B (published 2023), Report B says $5.1B (published 2024). How should the document-analysis stage hand this off to synthesis?

A) Output both values with full source attribution and publication dates, annotate them as conflicting, and let the coordinator decide reconciliation.
B) Report the more recent figure ($5.1B) and discard the 2023 value, since newer data supersedes older.
C) Report the average ($4.65B) as a single blended figure to avoid confusing the reader.
D) Report the higher figure ($5.1B) so the analysis does not understate the opportunity.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: A.** The analysis stage has no authority to silently reconcile a conflict of credible sources — it surfaces it **with attribution and dates**, and the coordinator does the reconciliation. The dated values show that this may be a **temporal trend**, not a contradiction.
B — *pick most recent*: dates are for interpretation, not auto-deletion; it loses a data point. C — *average*: fabricates a number that no one reported. D — *pick higher / not understate*: introduces bias, destroys provenance.
</details>

**Q3 · hard**
A synthesis agent must assemble a final report combining: quarterly revenue figures from three filings, a breaking-news item about a CEO change, and a list of technical benchmark results. To maximize trustworthiness and clarity, what should the report design do?

A) Convert everything to a single uniform prose format so the report reads consistently end to end.
B) Render financial data as tables, the news item as prose, and benchmarks as a structured list, while keeping each claim's source attribution and dates intact, and separating well-established from contested findings.
C) Merge all numbers into one combined table and drop the news item, since narrative text cannot be tabulated.
D) Pick whichever single filing is most recent for the revenue figures and summarize the rest into one paragraph.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: B.** Different content types are rendered in their own way (financial → tables, news → prose, technical → structured lists), provenance and dates are kept, and the report separates established vs contested. This is a direct requirement of Task 5.6.
A — *uniform format*: flattens structure, loses the precision of tables. C — drops credible content (news) and mixes incomparable data. D — *pick most recent* + summarization loss: loses data points and attribution.
</details>

### Tricky question **

**Q★★ (tricky):** A research subagent returns a clean structured payload: each claim has a `source_url`, a `document_name`, and a verbatim `excerpt`. The coordinator notices two credible sources report different churn rates and is ready to annotate the conflict with attribution. A reviewer says the design is complete. What is the *most important* remaining gap?

A) Nothing — claim-source mappings plus conflict annotation with attribution fully satisfy the requirement.
B) The payload omits each source's publication/collection date, so a temporal difference between the two churn measurements may be misread as a genuine contradiction.
C) The coordinator should immediately pick the higher churn rate to be conservative about retention risk.
D) The excerpts should be deleted to keep the synthesized report concise.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: B.** The design looks "complete" because the claim-source mapping and conflict annotation are in place — but Task 5.6 requires **four** things, and here the **temporal data** is missing. Without a `date` in the structured output, the coordinator cannot tell a real contradiction from an ordinary time difference (one source measured Q1, the other Q4). The missing dates are exactly the most treacherous gap: everything else looks correct, and it is easy to pick A.
A — falsely "complete": the requirement about dates is missing. C — *pick higher / not understate* archetype: the coordinator annotates, it does not pick. D — deleting the excerpts destroys the verifiability of provenance for the sake of "brevity" — the same summarization loss.
</details>

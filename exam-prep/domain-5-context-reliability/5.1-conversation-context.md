# 5.1 — Manage conversation context to preserve critical information across long interactions

> **Domain 5: Context Management & Reliability** (15%) · **Task Statement 5.1** | **Scenarios:** Customer Support, Multi-Agent Research | **What it tests:** where exact facts should live in a long conversation, how to beat "lost in the middle," and how to avoid bloating the context with "heavy" tool results.

## ⚡ TL;DR

- Progressive summarization is **lossy**: it blurs numbers, percentages, dates, order numbers, and customer expectations into vague prose. Keep exact transactional facts in a separate **durable case-facts block** that is injected into every prompt **outside** the summarized history.
- The model reliably reads the **beginning** (primacy) and the **end** (recency) of a long input, but "loses the middle" (**lost in the middle**). The cure is **structure**, not trimming: front-load the key findings + explicit section headers, not aggressive compression.
- Tool results pile up in the context and burn tokens out of proportion to their value (40+ fields when you need 5). **Trim** to the relevant fields.
- Between agents, pass **structured data** (key facts, citations, relevance scores), not verbose reasoning. Pass the full conversation history in every API request for coherence.
- Principle: fix the **root** (where critical state lives), not the symptom (how aggressively you summarize).

## 🧠 In plain words

> **Analogy:** 📋 meeting minutes: you keep exact figures word-for-word in a separate little table, and you don't hand them to the recap — the recap would blur them.
>
> Compression (summarization) is a "recap": it irreversibly erases exact numbers, dates, and order numbers ("15% discount" → "discussed pricing"). So keep such `case facts` as a word-for-word block **outside** the compression, in **every** prompt. And remember: in a 3-hour lecture people remember the intro and the finale, but lose the middle ("lost in the middle" — the model forgets what sits in the middle of a long input).
>
> 🚩 **Detector:** if the answer is "compress harder / raise the threshold / tweak the summarizer" for a context problem — that's symptom-not-root-cause; the right move is to restructure (summary at the **beginning** + explicit headers) and keep exact facts as a separate word-for-word layer outside the compression. A tool result with 40+ fields → trim to the needed 5.
>
> 🧷 **Reflex:** Numbers go in a table word-for-word, not in a recap. The important stuff goes at the beginning and under a heading. Compression as a context fix is a trap: rearrange, don't throw away.

## 📘 Theory

### Two layers of state: durable facts vs lossy narrative
Not all context is equal. The thread of the conversation can be compressed as much as you like — but exact facts must not be compressed. The architectural decision: split state into a **durable structured layer** (facts that must survive word-for-word) and a **lossy narrative layer** (narrative that can be compressed). Transactional facts — amounts, dates, order numbers, statuses, customer-stated expectations (`"the 15% discount I mentioned"`) — are moved into a **case-facts block** and included in **every** prompt **outside** the summarized history. Then they are available no matter how many turns have already been rolled up.

**WHY (root-cause-over-symptom):** summarization loses detail by its very nature. Improving the summarization prompt ("keep numbers verbatim") or raising the roll-up threshold from 70% to 85% are best-effort and delay tactics: at 20+ turns the facts will be compressed in the end anyway. What you need to change is **where the facts live**, not how aggressively they get rolled up.

### Lost in the middle — position as a design variable
The model's attention across a long input is **uneven**: strongest at the start and the end, weaker in the middle. When you aggregate ~75K tokens of subagent output, content in the middle ~50K loses out structurally — **regardless of relevance**. The cure is **structural**:
- **Front-load**: a key-findings summary at the beginning (uses primacy).
- **Section headers**: explicit headers index the middle so the model can "reach" it.

**WHY:** position is a first-class design variable. Aggressively squeezing everything down to 20K to "fit into the reliable zone" trades a positional problem (which can be fixed with structure) for irreversible content loss.

### Trim verbose tool outputs
An order lookup returns 40+ fields, but synthesis needs 5. The raw result piles up in the context and spends tokens out of proportion to its usefulness → **context rot** (accuracy and recall degrade as tokens grow). The fix: **trim the tool output to the relevant fields** before it settles into the history.

### Between agents — structured data, not verbose
The full conversation history needs to be passed in every subsequent API request for conversational coherence. But when the downstream agent has a limited context budget, the **upstream agent** should return **structured data** (key facts, citations, relevance scores), not verbose content and reasoning chains. You change the source, not the receiver.

#### "Token budget" — an explicit context budget

The term **"token budget"** from the exam Appendix is an approach in which the architect **explicitly limits** the volume of tokens an agent can use or pass on:

- **Upstream → downstream budget:** if the downstream agent has limited context, the upstream agent is modified to return a compact structured output (key facts, citations, relevance scores) instead of verbose reasoning — this is managing the downstream's "token budget."
- **Trimming verbose tool outputs:** trimming a tool's output to the relevant fields (keeping 5 out of the 40 order-lookup fields) before they accumulate in the context — preserves the budget.
- **Position-aware ordering:** a key-findings summary at the beginning of the aggregated input (primacy effect), section headers for structure — manages how the context budget is used by the model.

Reflex: "token budget" = deliberately design what, and in what volume, enters each agent's context. This is an architectural decision, not a setting.

## ⚠️ Traps and distractors

| Trap archetype | Why it's wrong |
|---|---|
| "Rewrite the summarization prompt: keep numbers/dates verbatim" | The summarization prompt is best-effort; as turns accumulate, even a careful summarizer drops details. The failure will return. This is a symptom, not the root. |
| "Raise the roll-up threshold from 70% to 85%" | Pure delay tactic: at 20+ turns the facts get compressed anyway. The threshold doesn't change the lossy nature of summarization. |
| "Compress all outputs to <20K to fit into the reliable zone" | Blanket compression throws away exactly the findings you need. Content loss can be worse than positional loss — and position can be fixed structurally. |
| "Put the critical stuff in the middle / stream in sequence / rotate who goes first" | Doesn't guarantee that critical facts land in an attended position. Within a single task the bulk is still in the "lost middle." |
| "Keep the full tool output (40+ fields) — it might come in handy" | Bloats the context, speeds up context rot, spends tokens out of proportion to relevance. You need to trim to the relevant fields. |
| "External retrieval triggered by phrases like 'as I mentioned'" | Fragile and expensive: depends on phrase detection, adds latency/failure modes, misses facts the customer didn't ask about again. A durable block is more reliable. |

## 🔑 Remember

- **Durable case-facts block** = amounts, dates, order numbers, statuses, customer-stated expectations → in **every** prompt, **outside** the summarized history.
- Summarization is **lossy** → tuning the prompt/threshold only **delays** the loss.
- **Lost in the middle**: primacy + recency are strong, the middle is weak → **front-load summary + explicit section headers** (structure, not trimming).
- **Trim tool outputs** to the relevant fields (5 of 40+) → fight **context rot**.
- The full history goes into every API request; a downstream with a small budget → upstream returns **structured data** (key facts, citations, relevance scores).
- **Token budget** (Appendix term) = deliberate management of what, and in what volume, enters the context; pattern: the upstream agent returns structured facts instead of verbose output when the downstream is constrained.

## 📝 Practice

*Answer it yourself first, then expand the "Answer."*

**Q1 · easy** — A customer support agent uses progressive summarization. Logs show that when customers reference a specific figure they stated 25 turns ago ("the 20% refund you confirmed"), the agent now quotes a wrong number; the detail was condensed into "discussed refund terms." What is the most effective fix?

A) Raise the summarization trigger from 70% to 90% so conversations have more room before condensing.
B) Rewrite the summarization prompt to instruct it to preserve all numbers and dates verbatim.
C) Extract transactional facts (amounts, dates, order numbers, statuses) into a persistent case-facts block included in every prompt, outside the summarized history.
D) Store the full transcript externally and retrieve it whenever the agent detects phrases like "you confirmed."

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: C.** Exact transactional facts must live in a durable structured block outside the summarized history — then they survive any amount of roll-up (root-cause fix). **A** is a delay tactic: at 25 turns the facts get compressed anyway. **B** — a best-effort summarization prompt is still lossy as turns accumulate; it fixes the symptom. **D** is fragile and expensive retrieval, depends on phrase detection, and misses facts that weren't asked about again.
</details>

**Q2 · medium** — A research synthesis agent aggregates ~75K tokens of subagent output into one prompt. It reliably cites the first 15K and last 10K tokens but consistently omits critical findings sitting in the middle 50K, even when they directly answer the question. How should you restructure the aggregated input?

A) Summarize all subagent outputs to under 20K tokens total so everything fits the model's reliable range.
B) Place a key-findings summary at the beginning and organize detailed results with explicit section headers.
C) Stream subagent results sequentially, finishing web-search results before introducing document analysis.
D) Rotate which subagent's output appears first across tasks so each source gets primacy equally over time.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: B.** Front-loading the findings uses primacy, and section headers index the middle — a structural cure for lost in the middle without losing detail. **A** — blanket compression throws away exactly the findings you need; content loss is worse than positional loss. **C** — streaming/ordering doesn't guarantee an attended position and creates a new "middle" for the document findings. **D** — rotation changes *which source* gets primacy between tasks, but within a single task the bulk is still in the lost middle.
</details>

**Q3 · hard** — In a multi-agent research system, the lead agent passes full conversation history to each downstream agent for coherence. Downstream agents now hit their context budgets because every order-lookup tool result carried by upstream agents contains 40+ fields and the verbose reasoning chains of the upstream agents, while only key facts and citations are actually used. What is the most effective change?

A) Add a separate persistent case-facts block to the downstream agents' prompts.
B) Stop passing conversation history to downstream agents to free up their context budget.
C) Modify the upstream agents to return structured data (key facts, citations, relevance scores) and trim tool outputs to the relevant fields, instead of verbose content and reasoning chains.
D) Increase the downstream agents' summarization aggressiveness so the verbose upstream content is condensed on arrival.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: C.** When the downstream is budget-constrained, fix the **source**: the upstream returns structured data (key facts, citations, relevance scores) and trimmed tool outputs, not verbose reasoning. This removes the bloat at the root. **A** — a case-facts block solves the loss of exact facts during summarization, but not the *bloat* problem from verbose output; it's the wrong tool. **B** — the full history must be passed for coherence; removing it would break that coherence. **D** — summarization on arrival is lossy and spends the downstream's tokens compressing what the upstream shouldn't have sent at all; it fixes the symptom, not the root.
</details>

### Tricky question **

**Q★★ (tricky):** A support agent keeps full verbose tool outputs (40+ fields per order lookup) in context AND uses progressive summarization at 70% capacity. Engineers observe two failures: (1) the agent quotes wrong refund amounts the customer stated 30 turns ago, and (2) context fills so fast that summarization triggers early and aggressively. A teammate proposes: "Just trim every tool output to its 5 relevant fields — that frees enough tokens to delay summarization, which solves both the bloat and the lost-numbers problem." Is this the most effective fix, and why?

A) Yes — trimming tool outputs frees tokens, delays summarization, and therefore preserves the customer's stated numbers as a side effect.
B) No — trimming tool outputs fixes the token bloat, but the customer-stated numbers must be extracted into a durable case-facts block outside the summarized history; delaying summarization only postpones their loss.
C) No — the real fix is to disable summarization entirely and keep the full verbatim history so no numbers are ever lost.
D) Yes — but only if you also rotate which tool result appears first so no single lookup dominates the primacy slot.

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Correct answer: B.** The trap is that the teammate's proposal solves **one** real problem (token bloat — yes, trimming verbose tool outputs to the relevant fields is right) and disguises that as a solution to the **second**. But customer-stated numbers are lost not because of a token shortage, but because of the **lossy nature of summarization**: "delay summarization" only postpones their roll-up — at 30 turns they get compressed anyway. The root is *where the facts live*: they need to be moved into a durable **case-facts block** outside the summarized history. So you need **two independent** fixes (trim + case-facts block), not one; the "side effect" in option A is false causality. **C** — disabling summarization is unrealistic: context is finite (context rot), and the history will overflow anyway. **D** — primacy rotation has nothing to do with rolling up exact facts and doesn't solve the loss of numbers.
</details>
